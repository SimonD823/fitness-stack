### ⚠️ The Critical Interaction: Sleeve Capacity + Event Day

The event day is where the sleeve becomes the most important planning factor. At 116 kg, you will burn an estimated **5,000–6,500 kcal** over 14–16 hours. To avoid bonking in the second half, you need to consume approximately **3,000–4,000 kcal during the event** — roughly one portion of food every 30–40 minutes throughout the day.

At 150–200 kcal per sleeve-sized portion, that means **20–25 small eating occasions** over 16 hours. This is physically achievable, but it requires:

1. **Starting earlier than you feel hungry** — eat from the first 30 minutes even if you feel fine
2. **Never skipping an eating window** — once you fall behind on a sleeve stomach you cannot catch up with a large meal
3. **Liquid calories are your friend** — sports drinks, milk, diluted juice, and protein shakes can be sipped continuously between solid portions and are much easier to consume than food when fatigued
4. **Practising this eating frequency during training** — the long Saturday walks from Week 5 onward must include regular small eating to train your gut for event day

**This is the single biggest event-day risk for a sleeve recipient: falling behind on fuelling and being physically unable to catch up.**

### ⚠️ Calorie Deficit and Training Load

**1,600 kcal is a healthy and appropriate weight-loss target** at your current weight and stage of post-bariatric recovery. Your estimated TDEE at 116 kg with moderate activity is approximately 2,600–2,800 kcal, giving you a deficit of roughly 1,000–1,200 kcal/day. At this rate you can expect approximately 0.8–1.0 kg of weight loss per week under normal conditions — healthy and sustainable.

**However:** On high-volume training days this deficit becomes too large and risks both bonking mid-session and impairing recovery. The solution is **not** to abandon your deficit — it is to increase carbohydrates specifically on training days and return to baseline on rest days. Weight loss continues; training days are simply fuelled adequately.

**Practical targets by day type:**

| Day type | Calories | Carbs | Protein | Fat | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------|---------|-----|-------|
| Rest day | 1,600 | 149 g | 150 g | 45 g | Full deficit — weight loss day |

**The sleeve constraint on fuelling:** To hit 2,600 kcal on a long training day you need approximately 13–17 eating occasions. Plan your training day eating schedule in advance rather than relying on hunger cues — post-sleeve hunger signals are not reliable guides to energy need.

#### The Core Recommendation: Tailwind as Primary Fuel, Not Secondary

Your original plan — SIS gels early, transition to Tailwind, possibly finish with Maurten — is on the right track but the priority order should be flipped. **Tailwind Endurance Fuel should be your core fuel from the start, not a later transition.** Here is why, specifically for your situation:

**Sleeve fit:** Tailwind dissolves in your bladder and you sip it continuously. Small constant doses hitting the sleeve rather than periodic boluses is exactly the right approach for a sleeved stomach. You are never swallowing 60 ml at once — you are taking 20–30 ml sips every few minutes.

**Sodium:** At 310 mg sodium per scoop, Tailwind replaces electrolytes automatically. A half SIS gel delivers only 5 mg sodium. Over 16 hours at 116 kg you will lose significant sodium through sweat, and cramping from sodium depletion is one of the most common reasons people stop in the back half of these events. Without Tailwind, you would need to carry separate salt tablets. With Tailwind in the bladder, sodium replacement is built in.

**Calorie continuity:** Because Tailwind is always in hand via the bladder hose, you will passively consume calories and electrolytes every time you take a sip — without having to think about it, open a packet, or schedule an eating occasion. This is significant cognitive load reduction over a 16-hour day.

#### The Half-Gel Calorie Problem

At a half gel every 30–45 minutes, SIS gels alone deliver only **59–88 kcal/hour**. At 116 kg you are burning approximately **320–380 kcal/hour** while walking. Relying on half-gels as your primary fuel creates a near-total energy deficit from the first hour. The plan only works if Tailwind is running simultaneously and continuously.

**Half gels are a useful supplement and texture change**, not a replacement for the bladder drink. Think of them as a flavour/texture break every 45–60 minutes, not the fuel source.

#### Maurten: Worth It or Not?

Maurten Gel 100 costs roughly 3–4× as much as a SIS GO gel per kcal. For a 16-hour event requiring ~30+ gel-equivalent doses, the cost adds up significantly. The genuine advantages of Maurten are:

- **No artificial sweeteners** — SIS GO contains Acesulfame K, which some post-bariatric patients find triggers bloating or GI discomfort. If you find SIS gels cause any stomach upset in training, Maurten is the clean-ingredient alternative.
- **Neutral taste** — avoids flavour fatigue over many hours. Tailwind's mild flavour already handles this well.
- **Hydrogel** — designed to be gentle on the gut. Relevant for standard athletes; with a sleeve and Tailwind as your primary fuel, this advantage is less critical.

**Verdict on Maurten:** Not essential if SIS gels agree with your stomach. If you have budget and want the cleanest ingredient option for gels, use Maurten in the back half (hours 8–16) when fatigue is highest and gut tolerance typically decreases. But test it in training first — the hydrogel texture surprises some people and a 16-hour event is not the time for a first encounter.

#### ⚠️ Critical Test: SIS GO and Artificial Sweeteners

SIS GO Isotonic contains Acesulfame K (listed as "Sweetener (Acesulfame K)"). Some post-bariatric sleeve recipients report sensitivity to artificial sweeteners — symptoms can include bloating, cramping, loose stools, or dumping-like responses. This is individual, not universal, but you need to know before event day.

**Action required:** In Week 2 or 3 of the training plan, use a half SIS GO gel during a treadmill session. If no GI reaction over the following 30 minutes, you are likely fine. Repeat on a long outdoor walk in Week 5. If you notice any discomfort, switch to Maurten gels (no artificial sweeteners) or SIS Beta Fuel (which uses a different formulation).

#### What to Avoid on Event Day (Sleeve-Specific)

- **Fizzy drinks** — carbonation causes rapid fullness and bloating in a sleeved stomach
- **Large volume drinks at once** — always sip, never gulp
- **High-fat foods** — slow gastric emptying increases nausea risk during prolonged exercise
- **Anything not tested in training** — event day is not a tasting session
- **Skipping doses because you feel fine** — post-sleeve hunger signals are suppressed further during exercise; feeling fine does not mean fuelled

#### Pre-Event Dinner (Thursday 27 August) — Sleeve-Adapted

Two small portions instead of one large meal:
- **6:00 PM:** Small bowl of white rice (~150g cooked) + small chicken breast, light seasoning. ~350 kcal
- **8:00 PM:** 2 slices plain white toast + honey + small glass of diluted apple juice. ~250 kcal
- Sip Tailwind Rapid Hydration (low-calorie, high-electrolyte) or diluted sports drink throughout the evening
- No alcohol, no high-fat foods, nothing new

## Recovery After the Event

**Immediately post-finish:**
- Carbs + protein within 30–60 min (recovery shake, chocolate milk, meal)
- Elevate legs
- Change shoes and socks

**Days 1–3:**
- Complete rest — do not try to "stay active"
- Eat to appetite
- Expect: significant DOMS (calves, quads, feet), elevated RHR, suppressed HRV

**Days 4–14:**
- Monitor HRV and Body Battery in Garmin Connect / Grafana dashboard
- Resume light walking only when HRV has returned toward baseline
- Treat like post-marathon recovery — full recovery is typically 7–14 days
